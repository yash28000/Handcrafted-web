About Aari

Aari, which is a form of hand embroidery, particularly the chain stitch, involves embellishing fabric using a needle with vibrant yarn, pearls, beads, quills, and sequins.
The plain fabric that needs to be embroidered is initially fastened firmly to the circular wooden frame. Depending on the demand and the goal, the majority of artists prefer silk, locally produced satin (Gajji). The artisan begins by using the usual Aari chain stitch. The thread is kept in place at the back of the framed cloth with a finger, and an Aari needle (Aar), which resembles a sharp-pointed awl, is held at the top. The Aari needle is pierced through the fabric after the craftsman begins stitching the design's outline in order to pull up the thread from the back of the framed cloth and strengthen the preceding stitch.
Depending on the fabric's design and thickness, many hooks are used to embroider it. The cobbler's stitch is very similar to these stitches, and the embroidery procedure is continued until the desired design is made on the fabric's surface. In order to enhance the artwork, sequin beads are stitched in as well

Story of Aari

Origin and history
Aari embroidery flourished during the twelfth-century Mughal rule, Traditionally, this embroidery was only done for the royals but it later travelled throughout India. Barabanki in Uttar Pradesh was the starting point for Aari embroidery. In Gujarat and Kutch, Aari was done by the Mochi Community. They were known as Mochi Bharat as they used to do this embroidery on leather juttis. Later they came to be known as Aari Bharat when they transitioned to doing this embroidery on clothes.
Aari comes from the word aar, which is the small hooked needle like a cobbler's awl. It is used to do this embroidery and bharat means to fill in, Aari is a chain stitch embroidery.

Process of Aari

Anatomy of the craft:

The CHAIN STITCH
The chain stitch is very prominent in Aari embroidery. It also consists of other variations like zigzag, double chain, chain with zigzag, etc. There are around 50â€“60 variations.

The design, consisting of various motifs, is traced on the fabric. After that, the embroidery is done using the needle. A concentric pattern is followed in this embroidery to create each motif. It is a tedious job that requires a lot of practice.
